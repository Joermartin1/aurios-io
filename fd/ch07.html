Chapter 7 — Semantic Search and Retrieval

Keyword search finds what you said. Semantic search finds what you meant. The gap between those two things is where most retrieval systems fail — and where AuriOS™ is designed to succeed.

7A — Embeddings, Vectors, and Semantic Search

The Retrieval Problem

Your memory system contains a KTM CAN bus resistor specification. You recorded it in November 2024. Months later you ask: "What did I settle on for the KTM termination?" The words in your query are not the words in the stored record. The stored record says "120-ohm resistor between CANH and CANL." Keyword search misses this entirely. The answer is there. The retrieval fails.

This is not an edge case. It is the normal condition of human memory. We remember what things mean, not the exact words we used to record them. A retrieval system that only finds exact words will fail constantly — not occasionally — when searching a personal memory store built from years of natural conversation.

Keyword search finds what you said. Semantic search finds what you meant. The gap between those two things is where most retrieval systems fail — and where AuriOS™ is designed to succeed.

What an Embedding Is

An embedding is a way of representing meaning as a location in space. Imagine a vast map where every piece of text has a specific address — and similar meanings are placed near each other. "CAN bus termination" and "end-of-line resistor" are neighbors on this map. They mean the same thing in the context of motorcycle electronics, even though they share no words. "My dog" and "quarterly earnings" are on opposite sides of the map. An embedding is the set of coordinates that places a piece of text at its precise location on that map — typically hundreds or thousands of numbers describing where the text sits in the space of all possible meanings.

Embeddings are generated by running text through a neural network trained specifically to place similar meanings close together. Once generated, they are stored alongside the original text. At query time, your query is embedded using the same model — producing its own set of coordinates — and the system searches for stored records whose coordinates are closest to your query's coordinates. This is semantic search: finding meaning by proximity in space rather than by exact word match.

The practical result is extraordinary. A query about "what I decided on for the motorcycle wiring" can retrieve a record titled "KTM 1190 CAN bus termination resistor — 120 ohm." No shared words. Completely different phrasing. Same meaning. Retrieved correctly.

How Retrieval Systems Are Built

Modern AI memory retrieval combines multiple techniques, each addressing a different aspect of the retrieval problem. Understanding how they work together explains why AuriOS™'s retrieval architecture is being built the way it is.

—FTS5 (Full Text Search version 5) — SQLite's native keyword search engine. Fast, deterministic, and already deployed in AuriOS™. FTS5 excels at exact and near-exact matches — finding records that contain the specific words you typed. It is the foundation of the retrieval stack and handles the majority of straightforward queries efficiently. Its limitation is the one described above: it cannot find meaning when the words don't match.
—Vector Search (ChromaDB) — A local vector database that stores embeddings and searches them by semantic similarity. ChromaDB is the planned addition to AuriOS™'s retrieval stack — open source, Python-native, and deployable locally at negligible cost. Once deployed, every record in the memory store will have an embedding, and queries will search by meaning as well as by keyword. The one-time cost to embed the full corpus is approximately thirty cents. The per-query cost of semantic search is effectively zero. The technology to make memory genuinely retrievable by meaning is not expensive. It is simply not yet deployed.
—Hybrid Search — The combination of FTS5 keyword search and vector semantic search, with results merged using a technique called Reciprocal Rank Fusion. A hybrid search runs both searches simultaneously, combines the ranked results, and surfaces records that score well on either or both dimensions. The best of both worlds: the speed and precision of keyword search for exact matches, and the meaning-awareness of semantic search for everything else. This is the target retrieval architecture for AuriOS™.
—Re-ranking — A second scoring pass applied after the initial retrieval. The initial search — whether keyword, semantic, or hybrid — returns a set of candidate records ranked by relevance score. Re-ranking evaluates each candidate more carefully, applying a more sophisticated relevance model to determine which records are actually most useful for the specific query. The difference between probably right and confidently right. Re-ranking adds a small cost but meaningfully improves the quality of what gets injected into the context window — which directly improves the quality of Auri™'s response.

Why This Matters for Personal Memory

A standard search engine retrieves from a corpus of billions of documents. It can afford to miss some results — there are always more. A personal memory system retrieves from your corpus. Missing a result is not an acceptable tradeoff. If the answer is in your memory, the system must find it.

This is why AuriOS™'s retrieval architecture uses all four techniques in combination rather than choosing one. FTS5 for speed and precision on exact matches. Vector search for meaning when words don't align. Hybrid search to combine both. Re-ranking to ensure the most relevant results reach the context window. Each layer addresses a failure mode the others cannot.

The data is there. The pipes to retrieve it fully are being built.

Exhibit for Nerds — Vector Mathematics

Embeddings are dense vectors in high-dimensional space — typically 768 to 3,072 dimensions. Similarity is measured as cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors. A cosine similarity of 1.0 means identical direction — perfect semantic match. A score of 0 means orthogonal — no semantic relationship. Hybrid retrieval combines BM25 probabilistic keyword ranking with cosine similarity, merged using Reciprocal Rank Fusion to produce a unified relevance score.

7B — How AuriOS™ Retrieves Memory — MQI™ and the Auri™ Tool Interface

MQI™ — Memory Query Interface

The MQI™ — Memory Query Interface — is the standardized interface through which any authorized caller queries the AuriOS™ memory store. Partner AIs, agents, external services connecting through PAIPy™, and AuriOS™'s own orchestration layer all access memory through the same interface. The design is deliberate and important.

The AI does not have direct database access. It cannot browse your memory store, modify records, delete entries, or exfiltrate data. It calls MQI™ functions — specific, bounded requests that return structured results — and receives back exactly what those functions are designed to return. The boundary between what the AI can ask for and what it can do to your data is enforced at the architectural level, not by policy.

This is the difference between a key that opens a door and a key that owns the building. The AI has the first kind of access. You have the second.

The MQI™ exposes five core functions:

—search_memory(query, project, type) — Searches the L1 hot tier for relevant memory records matching the query. The primary retrieval function for curated memory — fast, deterministic, and always executed on every Ask Auri™ query. Project and type parameters allow scoped retrieval when the context is known.
—get_project_context(project_name) — Returns the full project summary, current status, and key entities for a named project. Used when Auri™ needs comprehensive context about a specific project rather than individual memory records. The difference between asking "what do I know about KTM" and asking "give me everything about the KTM build."
—search_raw(query, project, date_range) — Searches the L3 cold tier raw corpus for deep retrieval. Slower than L1 search but reaches the complete historical record — every conversation, every message, every exchange, going back as far as ingestion reaches. Used when the answer is not in the curated tier and requires searching the full corpus.
—get_thread(conversation_id) — Returns the full content of a specific historical conversation. Used when a partial retrieval surfaces a relevant session and Auri™ needs the complete context of that exchange rather than individual extracted records.
—list_projects() — Returns all Member projects with their current status. Used by Auri™ to orient itself within the Member's project landscape before routing a query — understanding what exists before deciding where to look.

The Auri™ Tool Interface

The MQI™ functions are exposed to Partner AIs through the Auri™ Tool Interface — the boundary layer that governs what Partner AIs can and cannot access. When Claude, Gemini, or any other Partner AI needs to retrieve memory to answer your query, it does not call Custos Aurum™ directly. It calls a tool through the Auri™ Tool Interface, which in turn calls the appropriate MQI™ function.

This layered architecture matters for several reasons. It means that no Partner AI ever has direct access to your raw data. It means that access can be scoped — a Partner AI handling a specific query type can be given access to the relevant MQI™ functions and no others. And it means that as the Partner AI stack evolves — as new models are added and others exit — the interface to your memory remains consistent. The models change. The architecture protecting your data does not.

The KTM Problem — Solved

With hybrid search deployed against the full corpus, the query "What did I settle on for the KTM termination?" retrieves the November 2024 build session through semantic similarity — even though no words in the query match the stored record. The MQI™ search_raw() function searches by meaning rather than by exact words. The record surfaces. The answer that has been sitting in the cold tier since November 2024 becomes accessible.

This is not new data. The data was always there. The difference is the pipe to reach it. FTS5 keyword search handles queries where the words align. Hybrid search handles queries where the meaning aligns but the words do not. Re-ranking ensures the most relevant record surfaces at the top. The MQI™ delivers it to the context window. Auri™ responds as if it has always known.

Every Member has a version of the KTM problem — facts recorded months or years ago, in language they no longer remember using, about decisions they need to revisit. The retrieval architecture in 7A and the MQI™ architecture in 7B exist to solve that problem completely.

The Cost Reality

The technology to make personal memory genuinely searchable by meaning is not expensive. It is simply not yet standard.

Embedding a large personal corpus — the one-time process of generating semantic coordinates for every stored record — costs approximately thirty cents at current API rates. ChromaDB, the local vector database that stores and searches those embeddings, is open source and free. It runs on your local machine with no subscription fee and no ongoing cost. Per-query semantic search, once deployed, costs effectively nothing — the computation is local. Re-ranking, which adds the second scoring pass that separates probably right from confidently right, costs approximately seven dollars and thirty cents per year at typical usage rates.

The complete retrieval stack — hybrid search with re-ranking, deployed against a full personal memory corpus — costs less than a cup of coffee to deploy and less than a monthly streaming subscription to operate.

The reason it does not exist in other products is not cost. It is architecture. Most AI products are not designed around the premise that your complete personal history should be permanently, privately, and semantically searchable. AuriOS™ is designed around exactly that premise. The capability exists. The cost is negligible. The pipes are being built.
