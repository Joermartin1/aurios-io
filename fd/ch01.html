Part One — Where We Came From

Chapter 1 — The History of AI

The seventy-year story: the government agencies, the university labs, the political champions, the winters, the revivals, and the 2017 breakthrough that made everything you are reading possible.

CONFIDENTIAL · NDA-Gated · Founding Circle Edition · Joe R Martin · 2026

Most people had heard of the internet by 1996. Almost no one knew what it was. That is happening again right now — with AI. This chapter tells the seventy-year story: the government agencies, the university labs, the political champions, the winters, the revivals, and the 2017 breakthrough that made everything you are reading possible.

1.1 A Familiar Story

Most people had heard of the internet by 1996. Almost no one knew what it was.

The internet arrived the way all genuinely transformative technologies arrive — faster than culture could absorb it, stranger than imagination had prepared for and built by a small group of people who had been working on it for decades before anyone outside the room paid attention.

By 1994, Joe R Martin was one of the people in the room. He was running CyberSim — a Houston company he had founded in 1991 for commercial virtual reality production. CyberSim shared an office with their customer Gensler architects in downtown Houston at the time. Together they enjoyed a "T1" connection to the internet, the fastest typical internet connection of the time – 1.544 Mbps. CyberSim also had several Compaq Computer file servers on hand, a biproduct of Compaq (now HP) being CyberSim's largest customer. Then one day in 1994, the CyberSim team decided to plug in some modems and try selling internet to outside customers. Almost overnight, CyberSim became one of Houston's very first internet service providers (ISP).

It was a bet on technology and timing. What started in 1994 with a handful of users grew to over 50,000 and the largest ISP in Houston by the end of 1996 (growth >1000%) and one of the largest in Texas by 1998, when the company became public.

That is happening again right now. With AI. Thirty years later, almost to the month.

1.2 Sputnik and the Agency That Built the Future

The modern history of both AI and the internet begins on October 4, 1957 — not in a university lab, but in the night sky over the Soviet Union. Sputnik 1 completed its orbit in 96 minutes. President Eisenhower created the Advanced Research Projects Agency in direct response. ARPA had no laboratories of its own. It funded the smartest people it could find at the best universities and told them to work on whatever seemed most important. The oversight was minimal. The ambition was maximal. The budget was enormous.

ARPA would fund both the internet and artificial intelligence — often simultaneously, often through the same offices, often paying the same researchers. The two histories are not parallel tracks. They are the same track.

Part I: The Foundation Years

1.3 Licklider — The Man Who Imagined Everything

J.C.R. Licklider's 1960 paper 'Man-Computer Symbiosis' described a future in which humans and computers would think together. In 1962 he wrote memos to 'Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network' describing a globally connected system through which anyone could access any information from anywhere. When ARPA hired him to run its Information Processing Techniques Office, he brought that agenda with him. His philosophy: fund people, not projects. Under Licklider, ARPA funded Minsky, McCarthy, Simon, and Newell — the founding generation of AI research. This was the most consequential funding decision in the history of technology.

1.4 Dartmouth 1956 — AI Is Named

The term 'artificial intelligence' was coined at Dartmouth College in the summer of 1956. John McCarthy organized eight weeks of thinking about whether machines could be made to think. Programs solved algebra, proved theorems, played checkers. Herbert Simon predicted in 1965 that 'machines will be capable, within twenty years, of doing any work a man can do.' He was wrong. The gap between narrow competence and general intelligence turned out to be wider than anyone imagined. That gap would take sixty more years to meaningfully close.

1.5 ARPANET — The First Network

In 1966, Bob Taylor sat in his Pentagon office looking at three terminals connected to three different ARPA-funded computers. To talk to one institution he had to log off one terminal and into another. He lobbied ARPA director Charles Herzfeld for funding. The meeting lasted twenty minutes. Herzfeld redirected one million dollars from a ballistic missile program.

On October 29, 1969, the first message was sent between UCLA and the Stanford Research Institute. It was supposed to be the word 'login.' The system crashed after the first two letters. The first message ever sent across what would become the internet was 'lo.'

1.6 TCP/IP — The Protocol That Made Everything Talk

In 1974, Bob Kahn at ARPA and Vint Cerf at Stanford published the Transmission Control Protocol. TCP/IP is to the internet what the transistor is to computing — the foundational specification on which everything else was built. Every email ever sent, every webpage ever loaded runs on a protocol designed in the early 1970s by two people trying to get military packet radio networks to talk to university computers.

1.7 The First and Second AI Winters

Senator Mansfield's 1969 amendment required DARPA to fund only mission-oriented research. The freewheeling era of Licklider-style funding was over. DARPA withdrew broad AI support starting in 1974. The first AI winter began.

AI came back in the 1980s through expert systems. XCON saved Digital Equipment Corporation an estimated $40 million per year. Japan announced a ten-year plan to achieve general AI. The US responded with $600 million in DARPA funding. And then it collapsed again. Expert systems were brittle — extraordinary within their domain, useless outside it. The second AI winter began in the late 1980s.

1.8 The Gore Bill and the Commercial Internet

Senator Al Gore's High Performance Computing and Communications Act of 1991 allocated $600 million for high-performance computing. President George H.W. Bush signed it. One funded institution was the NCSA at the University of Illinois, where Marc Andreessen built Mosaic in 1993 — the first web browser displaying images alongside text. Congressman Rick Boucher's 1992 legislation opened the internet to commercial use. By 1994, websites were doubling every few months. Joe R Martin was running CyberSim, introducing email to everyone he knew, building infrastructure for customers who did not yet know they needed it. In 1996, the internet broke out into the mainstream.

1.9 Virtual Reality — Swallowed by the Internet

Ivan Sutherland built the first head-mounted display at Harvard in 1968 — so heavy it was suspended from the ceiling. Jaron Lanier coined 'virtual reality' in 1987 and founded VPL Research. By 1991 a commercial VR industry was taking shape. Joe Martin founded CyberSim that year. What nobody had fully reckoned with was the internet. The accessible, text-and-image web displaced the expensive, hardware-intensive proposition of VR. Recognizing that VR would need to be moved to the back-burner giving way to financial and personnel resources, CyberSim became PDQ.net to focus on continued customer expansion. The technology changed. The instinct to be early did not.

1.9A Virtual Reality — The Original Immersive Interface

The story of CyberSim is not just a footnote in internet history. It is a chapter in a larger story about how humans learn to interface with machines — and how the lessons of one technology era resurface, refined, in the next.

Virtual reality in the early 1990s was the most ambitious human-machine interface project ever attempted outside of government laboratories. The goal was total immersion: replace the human's sensory environment with a synthetic one. Not a window into a computer — a world inside one.

CyberSim in the New York Times, Sunday July 3, 1994.

The tools were extraordinary for their time and crude by any current standard. Head-mounted displays weighed eight pounds and tracked head movement at 30Hz — a latency enough to notice. Data gloves captured finger position through flex sensors and transmitted gesture data to drive virtual hands. Six-degree-of-freedom trackers followed every movement of the user's head and body in space. CAVE environments — Cave Automatic Virtual Environments — projected stereo imagery onto the walls, floor, and ceiling of a room, surrounding the user completely in rendered space. The audio was spatial — 3D sound that moved with the user, placed objects in specific locations in the virtual environment. When you reached for a virtual object, it sounded like it was where it appeared to be.

CyberSim's immersive "Time Traveler" created for Compaq's 1995 trade show displays.

This was not a research curiosity. It was a commercial industry with real customers. Gensler let their client's "walkthrough" their project before they were built. CyberSim produced Astronaut training modules for NASA. Compaq featured CyberSim's VR presentation at their major exhibits. The oil companies used VR to walk through reservoir models. The automotive industry used it for vehicle design review before physical prototypes were built. The military used it for mission rehearsal.

Joe founded CyberSim in Houston in 1991 to bring this technology to the commercial market — not the defense market, not the research market, but the real-world business market where the deals were in the tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars and the customers were people who needed to see something before they could approve it.

The team he built then is the team he is bringing forward now. The people who learned what immersion means, what interface means, what presence means — in the years before anyone else was thinking about those questions at commercial scale — are the same people who understand, better than most, what is about to happen with AI.

1.9B The Lessons That Carried Forward

VR taught lessons that did not become widely applicable until now. The first was about presence — the sense of actually being somewhere, not just observing it. Presence is not a feature. It is the goal. Every interface decision in a VR system was evaluated against one criterion: does this increase or decrease presence?

The second lesson was about FPS and image quality. In VR, the FPS, or "frames per second" is the seamlessness of live video that the computing engine could generate. Over 30 FPS and the user would not notice the "frames" as they changed from one to the next. Under 30 FPS and it was observable to the user and visually annoying. Image quality was directly related and a trade-off. The higher the visual reality – the lower the FPS. In 1995, what CyberSim did on Windows based Compaq PC computers was novel.

The third lesson was about input. The keyboard and mouse are adequate for navigating information. They are completely inadequate for navigating space. VR forced the development of new input paradigms — gesture, gaze, voice, position tracking — that are now the standard inputs of every spatial computing platform and many AI interfaces. When you speak to your AI, you are using an input paradigm that VR practitioners were testing in 1992.

The fourth lesson was about output. A screen is a window. An head mounted display (HMD) is a world. The difference between a window and a world is not technical — it is cognitive. It changes how the brain processes information, how attention is allocated, how memory is formed. The immersive experiences that are now being built with AI — spatial computing, mixed reality, AI-generated environments — are built on foundations laid by people who learned what immersion actually means in labs and company offices in the early 1990s.

VR was swallowed by the internet not because VR failed but because the internet succeeded faster. The hardware was too expensive, the software was too fragile, the content was too thin. The internet was cheap, fast enough, and had a billion people contributing content simultaneously. VR could not compete on those terms in 1995.

But the ideas did not die. They went dormant. And now — with AI as the intelligence layer, with spatial computing as the interface layer, with voice and gesture and gaze as the inputs — everything VR was trying to build in 1991 is being built again, properly, with the right tools. The pioneers with arrows in their backs in the 1990's are the ones who know what the arrows were for.

1.10 The Neural Network Revival

A neural network is a computing architecture loosely inspired by the human brain. Instead of following explicit rules — if this, then that — a neural network learns by adjusting the strength of connections between layers of simple processing units. Feed it an input, it produces an output, compare that output to the correct answer, measure the error, and adjust the connection weights slightly in the direction that reduces it. Do this millions of times across millions of examples, and the network gradually learns to recognize patterns that no human programmer could have written down.

The key mathematical insight that made deep networks trainable was backpropagation — a method for calculating how much each connection in the network contributed to the final error, and therefore how much each weight should change. In 1986, Geoffrey Hinton, David Rumelhart, and Ronald Williams published the paper that demonstrated backpropagation could train networks with multiple hidden layers. "Deep" learning — networks with many layers between input and output — became theoretically possible.

Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio spent the next two decades refining these ideas while most of the AI research community ignored them. Expert systems were the fashion. Neural networks were considered too slow, too data-hungry, and too unpredictable to be practical. LeCun applied his convolutional neural network to reading handwritten ZIP codes for the US Postal Service in the early 1990s — the first commercially deployed deep learning system. It worked. The field largely shrugged.

1.11 ImageNet, GPUs, and the Avalanche

The problem with neural networks in the 1990s was not the theory. The theory was right. The problem was that training a deep network required two things that did not yet exist at the necessary scale: enough data, and enough computing power to process it.

Fei-Fei Li solved the first problem. A computer vision researcher at Stanford, Li spent three years assembling ImageNet — a dataset of fourteen million photographs, each labeled by human annotators with what it contained. The scale was unprecedented. When she presented it to the research community in 2009, many of her peers thought it was unnecessary.

The computing problem was solved accidentally, by the video game industry. NVIDIA had spent decades building Graphics Processing Units — chips optimized for rendering the millions of simultaneous calculations required to display fast-moving 3D graphics on screen. It turned out that training a neural network required almost exactly the same kind of massively parallel arithmetic.

In 2012, Geoffrey Hinton's team at the University of Toronto entered a neural network called AlexNet into the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge. The previous best error rate was 26 percent. AlexNet scored 15.3 percent. The gap was so large that several judges assumed an error in the scoring. There was no error.

Within two years, every serious AI research lab in the world had pivoted to deep learning. The techniques that Hinton, LeCun, and Bengio had spent twenty years developing became the foundation of every major AI system built since.

1.12 The Transformer — AI's TCP/IP Moment

Every technology has a foundational specification — a document that defines the architecture everything else is built on. For the internet, it was TCP/IP, published by Kahn and Cerf in 1974. For modern AI, it was a paper published in 2017 by eight researchers at Google. Its title was modest for what it contained: "Attention Is All You Need."

Before 2017, the dominant approach to language AI was sequential. A neural network processed text one word at a time, left to right, carrying a compressed summary of what it had seen so far. The Transformer solved this with a mechanism called self-attention. Instead of processing tokens one at a time, a Transformer processes all tokens simultaneously — and for each one, calculates a weighted relationship to every other token in the context.

An illustration of main components of the transformer model from the paper "Attention Is All You Need," Google, 2017.

Three consequences followed from this architecture. First, it scaled. Unlike its predecessors, the Transformer improved predictably as you added more data and more computing power — the more you fed it, the better it got, with no obvious ceiling. Second, it parallelized. Third, it generalized. The same architecture that learned language also learned images, audio, and video.

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok — every large language model in existence today is a descendant of that 2017 paper. The eight authors — Ashish Vaswani, Noam Shazeer, Niki Parmar, Jakob Uszkoreit, Llion Jones, Aidan Gomez, Łukasz Kaiser, and Illia Polosukhin — are the Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn of modern AI.

A large language model — LLM — is a deep learning system trained on vast amounts of text to predict and generate language. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok are all LLMs. Chapter 3 explains how they work in detail.

1.13 2026 — The Thirty-Year Echo

ChatGPT launched November 30, 2022. One million users in five days. One hundred million in two months — the fastest consumer technology adoption in history. The reaction was the same as 1995: most people had heard of AI. Almost no one knew what it was. In 1996, that changed.

It is 2026. The breakout is happening. The window that opens once a generation is open again. The people in the room right now are the ones who will define what comes next.
